Project Objective

TooGood is a responsive web app that lets you instantly select one or more of the most popular diets: Vegan, veggetarian and fish. Because of the first filtering, a suitable recipe can be found faster and recipes that are not relevant are sorted out. In addition, leftover ingredients can be entered directly into the search bar to avoid food waste. The project was created entirely by myself during my intensive UI programme with CareerFoundry.

Prototype

Problem

In most cases, a user is looking for recipes that are prepared according to a specific diet. Today, many people follow a vegan, vegetarian or a combination of vegetarian and fish diet. In most recipe apps, you often have to search for one of the most important diets in a confusing filter function. Also many suggestions are made on the start page that not fit the desired diet at all.

Solution

I developed TooGood to provide a quick, specific and simple search. With the app, ingredients can be entered directly into the search bar, so leftover food can be made into delicious dishes and not thrown away.

Key Functionalities

  • Choose nutrition type
  • Enter ingredients
  • Enter leftover ingredients
  • Searching for recipes
  • Share recipe
  • Collect recipe
  • Cooking instructions

Design Process

I used the Design Thinking Process, so I was able to explore alternative solutions to a defined problem.

Competitive Analysis

To understand what solutions are already available to users and where the opportunities for improvement lie, I conducted a competitive analysis. The competitors I analysed were Chefkoch and MyNewRoots. Based on the results of the marketing profile, the overall strategy and the UX analysis I created a SWOT analysis. In this analysis, I focused on the opportunities.

Conducting User Research

In the next step, I conducted user interviews with three participants. I only chose persons who like to cook, so that I get as many meaningful opinions as possible. I asked the participants specific questions about their daily lives, their cooking skills and diets, and their technological habits. In this way I wanted to find out their needs and frustrations.

User Persona

To scale the user information from my interviews and to make design decisions, I crated three user personas based on their statements and wishes.

At any point in the design process, I can refer back to the user personas and ask the question:
Does my persona like this?
Does my persona get along with the feature?

MVP

On this basis, I created the JTBD and divided the statements into needs and wants . From three choosen JTBD „Needs“ I created a user flow for each, which I put together in one user flow in the last step.

JTBD Needs

"When I have ingredients left, I want to find recipes by entering the ingredients, so I can use up ingredients I have left."


"When I search for recipes, I want to set a form of nutrition, so I can cook special meals."


"When I search for recipes, I want to set a level, so I can cook according to my skills."


"When I search for recipes, I want to collect them, so I can cook them whenever I want."

JTBD Wants

"When I have ingredients that are about to expire, I want to get prepare suggestions, so I don't have to throw the ingredients away."


"When I look for a recipe, I want to get seasonal ingredient suggestions, so I can learn more about food."


"When I am searching for a recipe, I want to read reviews, so I can decide if the recipe is worth cooking."


"When I found a recipe I like, I want to share it with family and firends, so I can inspire them."

Hypothesis

We believe that people waste a lot of food, because they don't know how to prepare available ingredients. We hypothesize that with an app that allows to enter available ingredients and provide recipes on how to prepare them, would significantly reduce food waste. In addition to this, people would save money.

Hypothesis

We believe that more functions offer an added value to the user. Now, they can share and rate recipes and set even more specific filters for a better user experience.

Exploratory Sketching Screens

Then I created my first exploratory sketching screens with the crazy 8's method with pen and paper. I tested my favourite screens on three users who gave me important feedback, which I will use for the further design process.

User Testing

I tested my favourite low-fidelity wireframes on three users who gave me an important feedback. From this feedback I created an error rating and a result synthesis which I used for the next iteration.

Mid Fidelity Wireframes

The new thoughts and results from the user testing were implemented in the next iteration, the mid fidelity wireframes.
To define the primary and secondary actions I created mid fidelity wireframes. The primary buttons guide the user through the app. In this step, colours were used in the design for the first time.

Developing the visuals

The visual style of TooGood was determined after a preference test. It is inspired by modern and fresh colours, materials and product shapes to inspire cooking and create a bridge to modern sustainability. The style guide includes the typography, iconography, the colours and the logo.

Typography

The responsive web app TooGood uses the typeface Futura PT. Futura PT is a typeface that has power and is meaningful. It strongly supports the CTA buttons and can communicate messages well. It should support the idea of modern sustainability.

Iconography

TooGood uses clear icons. They are outlined. Selected icons displayed inverse. Icons help to communicate messages faster and more clearly. In the app, they support the idea of modern sustainability with the help of typography.

Colours

TooGood uses fresh and dynamic colours. The colour selection also encourages cooking and communicate positive vibes and bridge the gap to a modern and sustainable lifestyle. A clear colour selection supports the primary and secondary CTA buttons.

Logotype

The aim with the logo design was to create a casual and friendly image. The logo should promote the feeling of a more conscious and sustainable lifestyle. It originated from the German proverb: „Have you got tomatoes on your eyes?“ Which means something like „are you blind?“ Can't you see that the food is still good and can be used? To incorporate this thought, the O's were replaced by tomatoes.
